Workplace burnout isn’t a medical condition or disease, but it is widely recognized by psychologists and the medical community as a phenomenon that can very seriously affect our health and well-being. The World Health Organization (WHO) defines burnout as a syndrome resulting from chronic workplace stress, characterized by:

A few weeks or even a couple of months of major adjustments — like changing work, home, and school setups; new or modified modes of communication with colleagues; isolation; lack of privacy; and increased nonwork burdens, like childcare and other additional household responsibilities — can bring a level of stress. When those changes are short-term, they tend to be easier to manage, says Steginus..
